Overview

Wet’suwet’en territory has never been ceded to Canada or any other colonial power.

In 1997, a landmark decision by the Canadian Supreme Court would recognize that fact. The Delgamuukw vs. British Columbia decision would establish in Canadian law that Wet'suwet'en had never extinguished title to their territories and recognized their traditional system of law that predates the days of elected band councils enacted under Canada's Indian Act.

Years after the Delgamuukw ruling, a conflict between the Wet'suwet'en Nation and the Coastal GasLink Pipeline (CGL) would test the jurisdiction of Wet'suwet'en law and Canada's recognition of their traditional governance system.

The Coastal GasLink pipeline is a TC Energy natural gas pipeline under construction in British Columbia, Canada. Starting in Dawson Creek, the pipeline's route crosses through the Canadian Rockies and other mountain ranges to Kitimat, where the gas will be exported to Asian energy markets.

The Wetʼsuwetʼen (wet-SOH-ih-tin) are a First Nations people who live on the Bulkley River and around Burns Lake, Broman Lake, and François Lake in the northwestern Central Interior of British Columbia.

The Healing Center was built in 2015 as a way to teach the Wet'suwet'en community about their culture and connection to the land.

The Coastal GasLink Pipeline (CGL) is the latest in a series of efforts by the Canadian government to move natural gas to the Pacific Coast of North America.

The project runs through the traditional territories of the Wet’suwet’en First Nation and is opposed by the Nation’s traditional Hereditary Leadership.

To exercise their rights to the land, the Wet'suwet'en began re-occupying areas of their traditional territory. When the Coastal Gaslink Pipeline was proposed, the focus of this re-occupation campaign became to build camps along the pipeline's route. This delayed the construction of the natural gas project until a provincial court order gave the Canadian RCMP "legal standing" to clear the camps.

Kilometer 27

Chief Woos’ Cabin and Supporter Camp

The cabin at Kilometer 27 is the smallest Wet'suwet'en occupation and was initially built in the winter of 2020 as a meeting location for the Hereditary Chiefs. The cabin later served as a location where supporters could gather outside of the exclusion zone set up by the RCMP.

Wet'suwet'en Governance

While the Wet’suwet’en Hereditary System unanimously opposes the project, Coastal GasLink does have approval from the Wet’suwet’en band council.

The band system was established under the Canada’s Indian Act as a means to undermine the traditional governance structures of indigenous peoples.
